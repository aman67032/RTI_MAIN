Timeline of RTI Amendments

The Right to Information Act has undergone several amendments since its enactment in 2005, each aimed at refining its implementation framework.

2019

Right to Information (Amendment) Act, 2019

This amendment introduced significant changes to the terms and conditions of service of the Information Commissioners at both central and state levels.

Key Changes:
  • Changed the fixed 5-year term of Information Commissioners to "such term as may be prescribed by the Central Government"
  • Removed the provisions that made salaries of Information Commissioners equivalent to Election Commissioners
  • Gave the Central Government the authority to determine the salaries, allowances, and terms of service of Information Commissioners at both central and state levels
Read 2019 Amendment
2012

RTI Rules, 2012

While not a direct amendment to the Act, these rules replaced the earlier RTI Rules of 2007 and modified the procedural aspects of RTI implementation.

Key Changes:
  • Modified the appeal procedure to the Central Information Commission
  • Specified word limits for RTI applications and appeals
  • Provided detailed procedures for withdrawal of appeals
View RTI Rules 2012
2006

RTI (Amendment) Ordinance, 2006

This was one of the first attempts to amend the RTI Act after its implementation. However, the amendment was eventually withdrawn due to strong public opposition.

Proposed Changes (Not Implemented):
  • Exclusion of file notings from the purview of the Act
  • Exemption of examination processes from disclosure
  • Restriction on disclosure of cabinet papers
Read About 2006 Proposal
2005

Original RTI Act, 2005

The Right to Information Act was enacted on June 15, 2005, and came into force on October 12, 2005. It replaced the Freedom of Information Act, 2002.

Key Provisions:
  • Established the right to information for citizens
  • Created a mechanism for obtaining information from public authorities
  • Established Information Commissions at Central and State levels
  • Defined penalties for non-compliance

2019 Amendment: Detailed Analysis

Background

The Right to Information (Amendment) Act, 2019 was passed by the Parliament on July 25, 2019, and received Presidential assent on August 1, 2019. The amendment primarily focused on the terms and conditions of service of the Chief Information Commissioner and Information Commissioners at both central and state levels.

Section-by-Section Analysis

Original Provision Amended Provision Impact
Section 13(1): The Chief Information Commissioner and Information Commissioners shall hold office for a term of 5 years. The Chief Information Commissioner and Information Commissioners shall hold office for such term as may be prescribed by the Central Government. Removed the security of fixed tenure, potentially affecting independence of Commissioners.
Section 13(2): Salaries and allowances equivalent to the Chief Election Commissioner and Election Commissioners respectively. Salaries, allowances and other terms and conditions of service shall be such as may be prescribed by the Central Government. Removed parity with Election Commission, potentially affecting status and independence.
Section 16(1): State Information Commissioners to hold office for 5 years. State Information Commissioners to hold office for such term as may be prescribed by the Central Government. Centralized control over State Information Commissioners' tenure.

Arguments For and Against

Arguments in Support
  • Standardization of service conditions across statutory bodies
  • Correcting "anomalies" in the original Act
  • Rationalizing administrative structures
  • Bringing parity with other regulatory bodies
Arguments Against
  • Undermines independence of Information Commissions
  • Reduces statutory protection for Information Commissioners
  • Gives excessive control to the government
  • May discourage Information Commissioners from taking decisions adverse to the government

Expert Opinions & Legal Challenges

"The 2019 Amendment strikes at the very heart of the RTI Act by compromising the independence of Information Commissions, which are the final adjudicators of the RTI process."
- Former Central Information Commissioner, Shailesh Gandhi
"The amendment has changed the fundamental character of the Information Commissions from being independent quasi-judicial bodies to being effectively administrative tribunals under executive control."
- RTI Activist, Aruna Roy
"The amendment brings necessary parity in service conditions across various regulatory bodies and does not affect the functioning or independence of the Information Commissions."
- Government Representative Statement

Jairam Ramesh vs. Union of India (2019)

This petition challenged the constitutionality of the RTI (Amendment) Act, 2019. The petitioner argued that the amendments violate the basic structure of the Constitution by undermining the independence of Information Commissions.

Status: Pending before the Supreme Court

RTI Activists' Collective vs. Union of India (2020)

A collective of RTI activists filed a public interest litigation challenging the Rules framed under the amended RTI Act, alleging that they further dilute the independence of Information Commissions.

Status: Pending before the Delhi High Court

Department-Related Parliamentary Standing Committee on Personnel, Public Grievances, Law and Justice (2019)

Key observations from the report on the 2019 Amendment:

  • The Committee noted concerns about potential impact on independence of Information Commissions
  • Recommended that the government ensure that the status and independence of Information Commissions are not compromised
  • Suggested clear and transparent criteria for determining tenure and service conditions
  • Emphasized the need for consultation with stakeholders before finalizing the Rules
