Understanding RTI Exemptions

The Right to Information Act, 2005 is built on the premise of transparency and accountability in governance. However, recognizing that absolute disclosure may not always be in the public interest, the Act provides for certain exemptions under Sections 8 and 9.

The exemptions under the RTI Act are not absolute. They are subject to the overriding public interest disclosure test in Section 8(2), which states that information may be disclosed if public interest outweighs the harm to the protected interest.

Important Principles of Exemptions

  1. Exemptions are specific, not general: They apply only to the specific information mentioned in Sections 8 and 9, not to entire documents or records.
  2. Exemptions are not mandatory: The Act uses the term "may be denied" rather than "shall be denied," giving discretion to the PIO.
  3. Burden of proof: The burden to prove that information should be exempted lies with the public authority, not the information seeker.
  4. Public interest override: Even exempted information can be disclosed if public interest outweighs harm.
  5. Partial disclosure: If only a part of the record is exempt, the rest must be disclosed after severing the exempt portion.

Exemptions Under Section 8(1)

Legal Text: "Information, disclosure of which would prejudicially affect the sovereignty and integrity of India, the security, strategic, scientific or economic interests of the State, relation with foreign State or lead to incitement of an offence."

Key Interpretations:
  • The exemption must be applied narrowly and specifically, not as a blanket cover
  • There must be a demonstrable link between disclosure and potential harm
  • Generalized claims of national security are not sufficient without specific explanation
Landmark Decisions:

Sethi vs. Department of Defence (CIC/WB/A/2006/00383): Security concerns must be specific and concrete, not hypothetical.

PUCL vs. Ministry of Home Affairs (CIC/WB/A/2009/00931): Information about human rights violations cannot be withheld under the guise of security concerns.

Legal Text: "Information which has been expressly forbidden to be published by any court of law or tribunal or the disclosure of which may constitute contempt of court."

Key Interpretations:
  • There must be a specific court order prohibiting disclosure
  • General judicial processes not under confidentiality orders are not exempt
  • Court records that are public are not covered by this exemption
Landmark Decisions:

Rakesh Kumar vs. Delhi High Court (CIC/WB/A/2008/01259): Information about ongoing cases is not automatically exempt; there must be a specific court order.

Trehan vs. Central Information Commission (WP(C) No. 3673/2012): Judicial proceedings exempt only if there is an express prohibition on publication.

Legal Text: "Information, the disclosure of which would cause a breach of privilege of Parliament or the State Legislature."

Key Interpretations:
  • Limited to information that would cause an actual breach of parliamentary privilege
  • Routine parliamentary documents not subject to privilege are not exempt
  • The PIO must specifically identify which privilege would be breached
Landmark Decisions:

Agrawal vs. Lok Sabha Secretariat (CIC/WB/A/2009/00614): Information about MPs' attendance records does not breach parliamentary privilege.

Mishra vs. Rajya Sabha Secretariat (CIC/LS/A/2011/003227): Expenditure details of parliamentary committees are not covered by privilege.

Legal Text: "Information including commercial confidence, trade secrets or intellectual property, the disclosure of which would harm the competitive position of a third party, unless the competent authority is satisfied that larger public interest warrants the disclosure of such information."

Key Interpretations:
  • Not all commercial information is exempt; only that which would cause competitive harm
  • Public interest can override this exemption
  • Information about public procurement contracts is generally not exempt
Landmark Decisions:

Naresh Trehan vs. Rakesh K. Gupta (W.P.(C) 85/2010): Defined the scope of "commercial confidence" and "trade secrets" under RTI.

BSNL vs. Jyoti Swaroop Khanna (CIC/OK/A/2006/00386): Information about public contracts cannot be withheld as trade secrets once the bidding process is complete.

In the educational sector, several exemptions are commonly invoked:

Evaluation Processes [Section 8(1)(d) & 8(1)(e)]:
  • Internal working notes of examiners may be exempt as fiduciary information
  • However, evaluated answer sheets are not exempt (Supreme Court ruling in CBSE vs. Aditya Bandopadhyay)
  • Moderation processes may be disclosed after results are declared
Student Privacy [Section 8(1)(j)]:
  • Personal information of students is generally exempt
  • Information about scholarships, financial assistance may be disclosed in broader public interest
  • Aggregate data should be provided rather than individual identifiable information
Landmark Decisions:

CBSE vs. Aditya Bandopadhyay (Civil Appeal No. 6454/2011): Evaluated answer sheets are information under RTI and must be disclosed.

Prashant Kumar vs. Delhi University (CIC/YA/A/2016/001587): Information about admission criteria and cutoffs cannot be exempted as commercial confidence.

Balancing Test: Public Interest Disclosure

Section 8(2) of the RTI Act provides for a public interest override: "Notwithstanding anything in the Official Secrets Act, 1923 nor any of the exemptions permissible in accordance with sub-section (1), a public authority may allow access to information, if public interest in disclosure outweighs the harm to the protected interests."

Factors Favoring Disclosure
  • Promoting transparency and accountability
  • Revealing misconduct, negligence or corruption
  • Improving public participation in decision-making
  • Promoting public health and safety
  • Facilitating scrutiny of public expenditure
  • Revealing discrimination or unfair treatment
Factors Against Disclosure
  • Genuine national security concerns
  • Privacy of individuals would be compromised
  • Legal proceedings would be prejudiced
  • Commercial harm to legitimate business interests
  • Breach of confidentiality in fiduciary relationships
  • Information would endanger life or physical safety
The Balancing Test: Public Information Officers must weigh the harm that might result from disclosure against the public interest served by transparency. This is not a mechanical exercise but requires careful consideration of the specific circumstances of each case.
