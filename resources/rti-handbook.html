What is RTI?

Understanding the Right to Information and its significance

The Right to Information (RTI) Act is a landmark legislation that empowers citizens to request information from public authorities, promoting transparency and accountability in governance. Enacted in 2005, it has transformed the relationship between citizens and the government by giving people the legal right to access information held by public authorities.

Core Principles of RTI

The RTI Act is founded on the following core principles:

  • Transparency: Government institutions should operate in an open manner, with their decisions and actions visible to the public.
  • Accountability: Officials and institutions should be answerable for their actions and decisions.
  • Citizen Empowerment: Citizens have the right to know how they are governed and how public resources are utilized.
  • Democratic Participation: Access to information enables meaningful citizen participation in democratic processes.

RTI as a Fundamental Right

The Right to Information stems from the fundamental right to freedom of speech and expression under Article 19(1)(a) of the Indian Constitution. The Supreme Court has recognized that the right to information is an integral part of the right to free speech, as without information, freedom of speech and expression cannot be meaningfully exercised.

Key Point: RTI Is Your Right, Not a Privilege

The RTI Act doesn't create a new right but formally recognizes and implements an existing fundamental right. This means that accessing information is not a favor granted by authorities, but a constitutional entitlement of every citizen.

Significance in the Education Sector

In the education sector, RTI serves as a powerful tool for:

  • Ensuring fair and transparent admission processes
  • Monitoring proper utilization of education funds
  • Verifying compliance with regulatory standards
  • Examining evaluation procedures and results
  • Holding educational institutions accountable for their decisions
  • Uncovering irregularities in appointments and promotions
  • Ensuring implementation of scholarship and aid schemes

Delhi University Admission Transparency (2022)

In 2022, a group of students used RTI to uncover discrepancies in Delhi University's admission process. Their applications revealed that several colleges had not followed the prescribed reservation quotas. The findings led to the implementation of a centralized online admission portal with real-time quota compliance tracking, benefiting thousands of students from marginalized backgrounds.

Legal Framework & Evolution

The journey and structure of RTI legislation in India

The Right to Information Act, 2005 represents the culmination of a decades-long struggle for transparency in India. Understanding its evolution and structure is essential for effectively exercising your right to information.

Historical Evolution

The journey toward RTI legislation in India includes several important milestones:

  • 1975: Supreme Court recognized the right to information as part of the right to free speech in State of UP v. Raj Narain.
  • 1990: Grassroots movement for transparency began in Rajasthan with Mazdoor Kisan Shakti Sangathan (MKSS).
  • 1997: Tamil Nadu became the first state to pass an RTI law.
  • 2000: Freedom of Information Bill introduced in Parliament.
  • 2002: Freedom of Information Act passed but never effectively implemented.
  • 2004: National Advisory Council drafted a new RTI Bill.
  • 2005: Right to Information Act passed in Parliament and came into force on October 12, 2005.
  • 2019: RTI Amendment Act introduced changes to the terms and conditions of Information Commissioners.

Structure of the RTI Act

The RTI Act consists of 31 sections organized into six chapters:

  1. Chapter I: Preliminary definitions
  2. Chapter II: Right to information and obligations of public authorities
  3. Chapter III: The Central Information Commission
  4. Chapter IV: The State Information Commission
  5. Chapter V: Powers and functions of Information Commissions, appeals, and penalties
  6. Chapter VI: Miscellaneous provisions

Key Point: Framework for Educational Institutions

Educational institutions that are established, maintained, or funded by the government fall under the definition of "public authorities" in Section 2(h) of the RTI Act. This includes universities, colleges, schools, and other educational bodies that receive substantial government funding.

Key Amendments and Judicial Interpretations

Over the years, the RTI Act has evolved through amendments and judicial interpretations:

  • RTI Amendment Act, 2019: Changed the terms and conditions of appointment, salary, and tenure of Information Commissioners, giving the central government more control over these appointments.
  • Judicial Interpretations: Supreme Court judgments have clarified the scope of the RTI Act, including which institutions fall under its purview and what information can be sought.

State RTI Rules

While the RTI Act is a central legislation, each state has its own set of RTI Rules that specify procedural details such as application fees, formats, and appeal procedures. These rules may vary from state to state, so it's important to consult the relevant state rules when filing RTI applications with state public authorities.

Preparing Your Application

Steps to take before drafting your RTI application

Preparing thoroughly before filing an RTI application increases your chances of receiving complete and relevant information. This section outlines the essential steps to take before drafting your application.

Research and Information Gathering

Before drafting your RTI application, gather as much information as possible about the subject:

  • Identify the specific issue or problem you want to address
  • Collect any available information from public sources (websites, annual reports, policy documents)
  • Note specific dates, file numbers, or reference numbers if available
  • Research similar RTI applications and their outcomes if possible
  • Understand the organizational structure of the authority you're approaching

Identifying the Correct Public Authority

Sending your application to the right public authority is crucial for a timely response:

  • Determine whether the institution is central, state, or local
  • For educational institutions, check if they are established by central act, state act, or are privately managed
  • For private institutions, determine if they are substantially funded by the government
  • Identify the specific department or office that likely holds the information

Key Point: Substantially Funded Institutions

The Supreme Court has held that "substantially funded" typically means that the government funding is significant enough to substantially influence the functioning of the institution. This is generally considered to be funding of 20% or more of the total expenditure, though each case may be assessed individually.

Understanding Fee Requirements

Different public authorities may have different fee structures:

  • Central Government bodies: ₹10 application fee
  • State Government bodies: Check respective state RTI rules (ranges from ₹10 to ₹50)
  • Additional costs for photocopying (typically ₹2 per page)
  • Below Poverty Line (BPL) cardholders are exempt from fees
  • Payment methods vary (postal order, demand draft, court fee stamp, online payment)

CBSE Fee Structure Disclosure (2023)

A parent association in Mumbai meticulously researched CBSE's organizational structure before filing an RTI. Instead of addressing it generally to CBSE, they correctly identified the Finance Division as the department holding fee-related information. This resulted in receiving comprehensive data within 20 days, which revealed unjustified fee increases and led to a successful challenge of a proposed 40% hike.
