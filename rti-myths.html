Popular Myths About RTI in Education

There are many misconceptions about using RTI in education. We've compiled the most common myths and debunked them with facts and legal references.

RTI doesn't apply to private schools

Common Myth

Many believe that Right to Information Act only applies to government schools and colleges, leaving private institutions completely outside its scope.

The Fact

Private schools that receive substantial government aid fall under RTI Act's purview. Additionally, private schools are regulated by government authorities (like education boards) which can be approached through RTI.

Legal Support:

Section 2(h) of RTI Act defines "public authority" to include organizations substantially financed by government funds. Supreme Court in DAV College Trust case (2019) clarified this further.

Students can't see their answer sheets

Common Myth

Many examination authorities and educational institutions refuse to show evaluated answer sheets to students, claiming confidentiality or policy restrictions.

The Fact

The Supreme Court has clearly established that students have the right to access their evaluated answer sheets through RTI. Educational boards and universities must provide copies upon request.

Legal Support:

Supreme Court's landmark judgment in CBSE v. Aditya Bandopadhyay (2011) established that evaluated answer sheets are "information" under RTI Act. This decision has been consistently upheld in subsequent cases.

RTI filing is complicated and technical

Common Myth

Many people believe that filing an RTI application requires legal expertise, complex forms, and technical knowledge of government procedures.

The Fact

RTI applications can be written in simple language on plain paper. The law intentionally keeps the process simple. You only need to clearly specify what information you're seeking and include a small application fee.

Legal Support:

Section 6 of the RTI Act requires only that the request be made in writing (including electronic means) with reasonable details to identify the information. No specific format is mandated by law.

RTI filers face retaliation risks

Common Concern

Many teachers, students, and parents worry that filing RTI against educational institutions may lead to retaliation, affecting careers, grades, or treatment of their children.

The Fact

The RTI Act provides options for confidentiality and protection. You can file through third parties, use legal representatives, or file anonymously in some cases. Any retaliation based on RTI filing is illegal.

Legal Support:

Section 6(2) of RTI Act explicitly states that applicants don't have to provide reasons for requesting information. Additionally, Section 11 provides for third-party information procedures that can help protect the applicant's identity.

RTI responses take too long to be useful

Common Myth

A widespread belief is that RTI responses are too delayed to be useful for time-sensitive matters like admission disputes, examination issues, or scholarship applications.

The Fact

RTI Act mandates information delivery within 30 days (and 48 hours for life-liberty matters). For educational issues with deadlines, you can request expedited processing. Courts have upheld the right to timely information.

Legal Support:

Section 7 of RTI Act requires information to be provided within 30 days, or 48 hours when the information concerns life or liberty. Central Information Commission has penalized authorities for delayed responses in numerous cases.

First appeals are just a formality

Common Misconception

Many RTI applicants believe that the first appeal is just a formality and doesn't yield results, so they either skip it or file it without proper attention to detail.

The Fact

First appeals are critical and often successful, especially in education matters. The First Appellate Authority is a senior official who can reverse or modify the PIO's decision and often takes a more comprehensive view of the request.

Legal Support:

Section 19 of RTI Act establishes the first appeal process. Statistics show that approximately 30-40% of first appeals in education-related matters result in favorable outcomes for applicants.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about using RTI for education-related transparency

What information can I request through RTI in education?

You can request a wide range of information including but not limited to:

  • Admission criteria and selection process details
  • Fee structure breakdowns and financial statements
  • Teacher qualifications and appointment details
  • Infrastructure and safety compliance information
  • Evaluated answer sheets and marking schemes
  • Scholarship disbursement records
  • Implementation details of government schemes
How much does it cost to file an RTI application?

The application fee is ₹10 for filing an RTI with central government authorities. State government fees may vary slightly but are typically ₹10-₹50. Additional charges may apply for photocopies (₹2 per page) or other document formats.

Importantly, applicants below the poverty line are exempt from all fees. Just attach a copy of your BPL card with your application.

What happens if I don't get a response within 30 days?

If you don't receive a response within 30 days (or 35 days if sent by post), you can:

  1. File a first appeal with the First Appellate Authority of the same public authority
  2. If the first appeal doesn't resolve the issue, file a second appeal with the Central/State Information Commission
  3. The Commission can impose penalties on the PIO for delays without reasonable cause

Non-response is treated as a deemed refusal under the RTI Act and provides grounds for appeal.

Can minors file RTI applications?

Yes, minors can file RTI applications. The RTI Act gives the right to "citizens" without any age restriction. Students under 18 years can file RTI applications regarding their education, exams, or other matters.

However, some public authorities may request a guardian's signature or identity proof. In such cases, parents can co-sign the application or file on behalf of their minor children.

What information is exempt from disclosure under RTI?

Certain information is exempt from disclosure under Section 8 of the RTI Act, including:

  • Information that would prejudicially affect India's sovereignty and integrity, security, or economic interests
  • Information expressly forbidden by a court of law
  • Information that would breach parliamentary privilege
  • Commercial confidence, trade secrets, or intellectual property that would harm a third party's competitive position
  • Information available to a person in his fiduciary relationship
  • Personal information which has no relation to public activity or interest

However, information that cannot be denied to Parliament or State Legislature cannot be denied to any person.
